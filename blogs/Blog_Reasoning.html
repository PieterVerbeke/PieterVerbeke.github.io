Thinking Fast and Slow—In Brains and Machines

#AI #CognitiveScience #ThinkingFastAndSlow #LLM #Reasoning

When Human Fallacies Meet AI Flaws

Two weeks ago, I attended a show by Inti De Ceukelaire titled Artifhacking Intelligence. It was a clever and entertaining take on how AI can be tricked—or “hacked”—to make mistakes. But what stood out most was how many of these so-called "AI weaknesses" closely mirrored the quirks and fallacies of human thinking.

Take this example: if you ask an AI to choose a number between 1 and 10, it will almost always say 7. That seems oddly specific—until you try the same experiment with a room full of people. Ask them to pick a number between 1 and 10, then ask who picked 7, and a large portion will raise their hand. Why? Because the number 7 is everywhere: 7 days of the week, 7 deadly sins, 007… This makes it top of mind for many people.

It’s fascinating how often we criticize AI for being imperfect while forgetting that humans aren't perfect either. Once again, understanding how the human brain works can help us design better AI.

For instance, the rise of so-called "reasoning models” is directly inspired by insights from psychology. These models are being developed to mimic not just the output of human thinking, but the process. With the Nobel Prize-winning theory of Thinking Fast and Slow by Kahneman and Tversky as a key influence, reasoning models use internal monologue to think “slow” and solve problems step-by-step

Two-System Thinking: How psychological theory drives AI development

The work of Kahneman and Tversky received the Nobel Prize in Economics because they demonstrated that humans are not purely rational beings but are subject to a host of cognitive biases and logical fallacies. More specifically, they argued that we have two modes of thinking:

System 1: Fast and intuitive

System 1 is quick, automatic, and effortless. It helps us handle everyday decisions like:

  • “What’s 2 + 2?”
  • “Is that a dog or a cat?”
  • “Would that food taste good?”

It’s efficient, but often prone to error when deeper thinking is required.

A classic example is this math puzzle:

If John bought a baseball bat and a ball for 1.10 euros, and the bat costs 1 euro more than the ball, how much did the ball cost?

A lot of people instinctively answer 10 cents -potentially even with the same confidence as an AI system would. However, the correct answer is 5 cents. System 2 thinking is required to get it right.

System 2: Slow and deliberate

System 2 is slow, deliberate, and effortful. It kicks in when we do complex reasoning, solve puzzles, or analyze data. But activating it requires conscious effort—and we don’t always make that effort when we should.

Language Models: Fast Thinkers by Default

Earlier versions of ChatGPT behave very much like System 1 thinkers. They’re trained to predict the most likely next word based on massive text datasets.

This makes them extremely fluent but not always logically accurate.

However, with the right prompt, these models can mimic System 2 behavior. Ask your model to Think step by step or Reflect before answering.

Modern reasoning models take this one step further. They now default to step-by-step processing. While it’s not true human reasoning, it demonstrates that language models can be nudged into more analytical behavior.

The Blurry Line Between Intuition and Reasoning

In both humans and machines, the line between fast intuition and slow reasoning isn’t always clear. Sometimes we believe we’re being logical when we’re simply rationalizing a gut reaction. Sometimes AI surprises us with what seems like genuine insight—when it’s really just surfacing patterns.

This overlap is fascinating—and critically important. Especially when AI is used in high-stakes decisions.

Should we trust its fluency? Or demand transparency into how it “thinks”? Should we rely on its System 1-like responses—or build new tools that favor System 2-style reasoning?

Either way, it’s yet another reminder that understanding human intelligence teaches us about AI—and vice versa.
